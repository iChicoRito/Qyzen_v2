# Taking an Assessment: The Student Experience

This guide walks through everything a student sees and does while taking an assessment, from the moment the assessment opens until they finish and view their results. It is written for a general audience — no technical background needed.

---

## Overview

When a student takes an assessment, they move through a focused, timed screen that shows their questions, keeps their answers saved as they work, watches for behavior that could compromise fairness, and finally shows them how they did. The sections below follow that journey step by step.

---

## What the Screen Looks Like

The assessment screen is intentionally stripped down and centered on the work at hand. Here's how it's arranged:

- **No side navigation.** The usual menu that runs down the side of the app is hidden during an assessment, so the whole width of the screen belongs to the questions.
- **A header panel at the top.** The assessment's key details (code, subject, section, term, educator, and schedule) sit in a panel at the top of the page, with the short instructions and a **Save Progress** button nearby. A small toggle lets the student switch between the List and Slideshow layouts.
- **The questions in the middle.** Each question sits inside its own bordered card, numbered and tagged with its type. In List view these cards stack down the page; in Slideshow view a single card is centered with navigation buttons beneath it.
- **Answer options laid out for easy scanning.** Multiple-choice options appear as tappable cards. On wider screens they arrange into two side-by-side columns; on narrow screens (like a phone) they stack into a single column so each option stays large and easy to tap.
- **A floating timer.** The countdown sits in a small panel that floats over the top-right corner of the screen, above the questions. It's a comfortable size on a computer and a bit more compact on a phone, and the student can drag it elsewhere or hide it entirely (details below).
- **Dialogs that take over the screen when needed.** Warnings, the submit confirmation, and the "Time's Up!" notice appear as pop-up boxes in the center of the screen that gently dim everything behind them, so the student's attention goes to the message.

The layout adapts to the size of the device: on a phone or small window, elements shrink and stack vertically; on a laptop or desktop, they spread out to use the extra width. (Making the window *too* small, however, is treated as a fairness concern — see the integrity rules below.)

## Getting Started

When a student opens an assessment, the screen leads with the key details so they know exactly what they're about to take:

- The assessment code, subject, section, and academic term
- The name of the educator who created it
- The open and close schedule (when it becomes available and when it locks)
- A short instruction reminding them to answer carefully, and that their answers are saved while they work

There is no separate "click here to begin" gate — the assessment is ready to work on as soon as it opens. Before the screen ever appears, the system quietly checks that the student is allowed to be there. To take the assessment, the student must be enrolled in that subject and section, the assessment must currently be open (or the student must have been individually granted access), and there must be attempts remaining. If any of these aren't met, the student is redirected — either to their existing results, or back to their list of assessments — rather than into a session they can't take.

To help students concentrate, the usual side navigation is hidden during the assessment, leaving a clean screen focused entirely on the questions.

---

## Viewing and Answering Questions

Students can choose how they'd like to see their questions, and can switch between the two layouts at any time:

- **List view** — every question is shown on a single scrolling page, so students can move through them all by scrolling.
- **Slideshow view** — one question is shown at a time, with a counter that reads "Question X of Y" so students always know where they are.

Questions come in two forms, each with its own way of answering:

- **Multiple choice** — the options are shown as clearly labeled cards (A, B, C, D). The student picks one, and their chosen option is highlighted.
- **Identification** — the student types a short answer into a text box.

If the educator turned on shuffling, both the order of the questions and the order of the multiple-choice options are mixed up. This shuffled order stays the same for the rest of that attempt, even if the student refreshes the page — so they never lose their place.

Depending on the educator's settings, the assessment may also show occasional **hints** that appear at random points during the session.

---

## Moving Through the Assessment

How students navigate depends on the layout they're using:

- In **slideshow view**, they move with **Previous** and **Next** buttons. Previous is unavailable on the first question, and on the last question the Next button is replaced by the **Submit** button. A running "Question X of Y" counter shows overall progress.
- In **list view**, students simply scroll through all the questions, with a Submit button waiting at the bottom.

It's worth noting what the assessment does *not* offer: there is no way to flag or bookmark a question to come back to it later, and no clickable map that jumps directly to any question. Students move through their questions in order (by scrolling or by stepping forward and back).

---

## Time and Auto-Saving

A countdown timer is always visible so students know exactly how much time they have left. The timer is flexible and student-friendly:

- It can be **dragged** to a different spot on the screen if it's in the way.
- It can be **hidden or shown** at will.
- Its color changes as time runs down — from green, to yellow, to red — and it gently shakes in the final stretch to draw attention. It also displays how many warnings the student has remaining.

Students don't have to worry about losing their work:

- Answers **save automatically** in the background shortly after the student stops typing or selecting.
- A manual **Save Progress** button is also available for peace of mind.
- If a student leaves and comes back — refreshing the page, closing and reopening the tab, or recovering from a brief internet drop — their attempt picks up where it left off. Their saved answers return, and the timer continues from where it was (time that passed while they were away still counts).

When the timer reaches zero, the assessment **submits automatically** and the student sees a "Time's Up!" message before being taken to their results.

---

## Staying Fair: Integrity Rules

To keep assessments fair for everyone, the screen actively limits and watches for actions that could be used to cheat. From the student's point of view:

- **Copying, cutting, pasting, and right-clicking** are blocked on the assessment.
- **Common inspect and shortcut keys** are blocked, and the system notices if browser developer tools are opened.
- **Leaving the assessment** — switching to another browser tab, minimizing the window, clicking away to another app, or moving the mouse off the page — is detected.
- **Shrinking the window too small** or arranging it side-by-side with another window (split-screen) is flagged.
- Pressing the **Print Screen** key briefly blanks the screen.

Each time one of these happens, a **warning message** appears explaining what was detected and how many warnings the student has left. Warnings add up, and if the student reaches the limit the educator set, the assessment is **submitted automatically and immediately** with whatever answers are in place at that moment.

On touch devices (phones and tablets), the checks that don't make sense for those devices are skipped, so students aren't warned for things they couldn't do anyway.

---

## Submitting

When a student is ready to finish, selecting **Submit** opens a confirmation step so nothing is turned in by accident:

- If some questions are still unanswered, the message points this out with a count and offers a **Submit Anyway** choice, alongside the option to go back.
- If everything is answered, the message simply reminds the student that once submitted, their score is final.

After confirming, the assessment is graded right away and the student is taken to their results screen.

---

## Your Results

Once submitted, students land on a results screen. On wider screens it's arranged as two panels side by side: a narrower **summary panel on the left** and a wider **details panel on the right**. On smaller screens these stack, with the summary on top. The results give a full picture of how they did.

The **left summary panel** leads with the score:

- A **score dial** showing their percentage, along with how many answers were correct versus incorrect
- Their **raw score** (for example, correct answers out of total)
- Their **best score** across all of their attempts
- Whether they **passed or failed** (the passing mark is 75%)
- The educator's name, the time they submitted, the time limit, whether shuffling was on, and how many warnings they used
- The assessment's open and close schedule

The **right details panel** holds the deeper review:

- An **attempt history** listing every submitted attempt with its score and pass/fail result.
- A **question-by-question review**. If the educator allowed review, students see the correct answers alongside their own, with clear right-and-wrong coloring. If review isn't allowed, students still see their own answers and whether each was correct, but not the answer key.

---

## Retaking

Whether a student can try again depends on the educator's settings:

- A **Retake Assessment** button appears only if attempts remain — this includes the retakes the educator allows for everyone, plus any extra attempts granted to that student individually. Choosing it starts a fresh attempt.
- If no retakes are left, students instead see a **Back to Assessments** button that returns them to their list of assessments.

---

That completes the student's journey — from opening the assessment, through answering and managing their time, staying within the fairness rules, submitting, and finally reviewing their results and any option to try again.
